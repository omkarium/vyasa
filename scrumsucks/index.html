SCЯUM SUCKS: WHY THE WORLD'S MOST POPULAR 'AGILE' FRAMEWORK IS AN UTTER FAILURE

In this post, I'm not here to bore you with definitions. I'm assuming you already speak the cult-like language of Scrum: Sprint, Velocity, Capacity, Story Points, Backlog, and the dreaded Events. You know the lingo, and if you're reading this, you probably also know the soul-crushing reality behind it.

If you still genuinely believe Scrum is the salvation of software development, prepare for a rude awakening. I'm here to tell you why Scrum is an utter failure and how projects that adopt it are often destined for disaster, regardless of their "end state." The fact is, Scrum sucks. Just accept it.

The biggest tragedy is that most teams aren't even doing true Scrum. They're doing "Scrum-but," a Frankenstein's monster of misused jargon and enforced ceremony. This distorted version sucks the life essence out of the workforce, quickly becoming a hostile playground where mid-level managers and Scrum Masters, utterly clueless about the technology and process, play god—a glorified delivery problem wrapped in a rigid, process-heavy bow.

Problem 1: The Micro-Management Incubator

The greatest failure of Scrum is how quickly it becomes a fertile ground for micro-management to thrive. Scrum stops favoring features and product value and starts focusing obsessively on its process and the time-boxed nature of achieving things. You end up with process on top of process on top of process, all just to get a single feature out the door. Everything is reduced to a card to be ticked by the end of the sprint. But why?

The Daily Stand-Up: The Ritual of Mistrust

The primary reason is simple: the "Scrum management" essentially does not trust its core team to manage their own damn work. Because a small percentage of people can't self-manage, the entire team is subjected to the same ritualistic punishment: the Daily Stand-Up.

  • The Three Questions are a Joke: We're supposed to answer: 1) What did you do yesterday? 2) What will you do today? 3) What will you do tomorrow? This forces the developer to pre-package their thoughts and plan a day in advance before making any meaningful progress today.
  • The 15-Minute Myth: The meeting is supposed to be no more than 15 minutes, but for some reason, it inevitably goes beyond 30 minutes, turning into deep-dive explanations of "why and how" from each team member, eating up everyone's most productive morning time. We're having meetings for the sake of meeting.
  • Backwards Timing: A stand-up at the very beginning of the day is fundamentally bad. Most developers need a moment to check logs, review yesterday's work, and get clarification from their leads or colleagues before they can provide a meaningful update. The early-morning stand-up forces you to give an update on a task that you haven't even properly started or planned for that morning.

The Echo Chamber of Retrospectives

The Retrospective—the theoretical cornerstone of continuous improvement—quickly devolves into an echo chamber. People are too afraid to talk about real, systemic change. Those critical discussions that should take place in a quick 5-minute side chat are intentionally pushed into a separate, agenda-driven meeting on the calendar sometime next week. Management claims we're being productive while making us the least productive and approachable people in the office.

Problem 2: Story Points and the Hostility of Estimation

Scrum becomes extremely hostile towards its team, especially when someone else decides the Story Points for a task that only the developer truly understands. It gets worse when:

The Story Point/Time Fallacy

The single most destructive and common mistake is when Scrum Masters and managers equate one Story Point to a fixed number of hours. You might have heard them say, "1 Story Point is equal to 4 hours." This is an absolute corruption of the entire concept.

  • Story Points are Relative Effort: A Story Point is supposed to be a relative measure of effort and complexity, not a timer! They look at past velocity and assume that number is a concrete, predictive constant for every future sprint, regardless of the work's nature.
  • Inaccurate Averaging: This metric becomes meaningless when teams are diverse (Problem 4). A task estimated at "1 Point" by a CRM developer might take them 4 hours, but the same "1 Point" task for an Integration developer might take 10. The team's velocity is averaged and normalized, making it an inaccurate, punishing metric for everyone.

The Overloaded 'User Story'

The foundational unit of work, the User Story, is supposed to be small, testable, potentially deployable, and value-driven. In practice, a User Story suddenly becomes not just one feature, but a set of features that are all crammed to be "finished" within that single sprint.

As an example, you get a 14-point story. That size itself is a red flag that it could be broken down. But it gets worse: the estimate rarely accounts for the inevitable external dependencies—the people up the ladder or operating in a different time zone that you are waiting on. If a dependency stops you, the story spills over. The original Story Points don't change, and it's portrayed as the developer’s mistake or incompetence. When you build a product with such concrete, inflexible rules that resist change, quality rapidly deteriorates, and teams crumble under pressure.

Agile is supposed to nurture creativity and flexibility. Scrum, as practiced, chains its users in an invisible jail driven by a myriad of arbitrary processes and rules with little to zero scope for actual flexibility or creativity.

Problem 3: The Ill-Equipped Scrum Master

Perhaps the most baffling decision in the Scrum process is electing Scrum Masters with little to no knowledge of their teams' tech stack, capabilities, or SLDC.

Their only goal is to get a story/card ticked ASAP while simultaneously over-promising to stakeholders, often when they absolutely don't need to. To them, it's all about velocity, the burndown chart, and vague metrics like story points, all in a frantic effort to normalize people and performance into mere data points on a sheet.

Competition, Not Collaboration

They champion those who finish tasks quicker, creating an artificial sense of competition and stress, rather than fostering curiosity, quality, and delight in the work. This is not right. Not everyone is made equal. Some people take more time than others, and the complexity of work varies wildly. If everyone is expected to work at the same velocity, why do we even pretend to have things like "competitive pay" or performance reviews? The framework is used as a **blunt instrument to manage people**, not a flexible tool to manage work.

Problem 4: Scrum's Resistance to Change (The Irony)

Scrum boasts that it is a revolutionary agent of change and adaptation. The painful, ironic truth is that, in practice, it becomes incredibly hard to implement fully and rapidly becomes very much against any real change.

Any deviation from the process, even if it demonstrably improves speed or quality, is flagged as "not being Scrum." Instead of inspecting and adapting to the team's needs, the team is forced to adapt to the framework's dogma.

This is the ultimate paradox: a framework claiming to embody agility that, through its rigid, dogmatic application by ill-equipped practitioners, forces teams into a state of organizational paralysis.

You might wonder, isn't this all just bad management, incorrect usage, and poor implementation?

Yes, it is. But when the system is so easily, so widely, and so consistently abused to become a tool for micro-management and performance policing—as 90% of implementations demonstrate—the problem is no longer the user. The problem is the product itself. The fact that the official framework is so easily corrupted into a nightmare scenario is its most damning indictment.

Teams and organizations which follows Scrum do not deserve to claim they are the best. They are living in their self created La-La Land.
